How to Get an A* in A-Level Law

Howard Chan (IB 45, Cambridge HSPS) shares his specific, actionable strategies for achieving an A* in A-Level Law. Learn exam technique, revision tips, and common pitfalls.

Getting an A* in A-Level Law isn't just about memorizing statutes and cases; it's about understanding the nuances of legal reasoning and applying them with precision under exam conditions. As someone who navigated the international school system in Tokyo, balancing IB demands with UK university applications, I know the pressure to perform. This guide isn't generic advice; it's the exact strategy I would have used, drawing on my experience with similar essay-based, time-pressured exams.

My journey to Cambridge for HSPS, with offers from HKU and HKUST, involved honing my analytical and argumentative skills – precisely what A-Level Law demands. Whether you're aiming for Oxbridge, a Russell Group university, or a top institution in Hong Kong, an A* in Law significantly strengthens your academic profile. Let's break down how to achieve it, focusing on actionable steps rather than vague platitudes.

Mastering the Core Content: Beyond Rote Learning

The A-Level Law syllabus (whether AQA, Edexcel, or OCR) is extensive, covering areas like Criminal Law, Tort Law, Contract Law, and the English Legal System. Simply memorizing case names and statutory provisions won't cut it for an A*. You need to understand the 'why' behind the 'what'. For example, don't just know the elements of negligence from Donoghue v Stevenson; understand the policy reasons for the neighbour principle and how it evolved through subsequent cases like Caparo Industries v Dickman.

Create detailed, structured notes that link concepts. Mind maps are excellent for showing connections between different legal principles and cases. For each major topic, identify key statutes, landmark cases, and relevant legal principles. Crucially, understand the *ratio decidendi* (reason for the decision) of each case, not just the outcome. This deep understanding is what allows you to apply the law effectively to new scenarios.

Developing Precision in Legal Application (Problem Questions)

Problem questions are where many students lose marks. The key is to use the 'IRAC' method (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion) rigorously. First, identify all relevant legal issues presented by the facts. Don't miss subtle points. Second, state the relevant legal rule(s) clearly, citing statutes and cases accurately. Third, and most critically, *apply* the law to the specific facts of the problem. This isn't just restating the law; it's explaining *how* the law applies, or doesn't apply, to the given scenario.

For example, if discussing mens rea in a criminal law problem, don't just define 'intention'; analyze whether the defendant's actions in the scenario meet the legal definition of direct or oblique intention, referencing cases like Woollin. Always consider alternative interpretations and counter-arguments, even if you ultimately dismiss them. Conclude by advising the parties on their likely legal position, acknowledging any uncertainties. Practice is paramount here; work through past paper problem questions meticulously.

Crafting Persuasive Legal Arguments (Essay Questions)

Essay questions require a different skill set: critical analysis and evaluation. Here, you're not just applying the law but critically assessing its effectiveness, fairness, or consistency. A common mistake is to simply describe the law. Instead, you must engage with the question's premise. For instance, if asked to 'critically evaluate the current law on consent in non-fatal offences', don't just list the rules; discuss their strengths, weaknesses, and potential reforms, referencing academic commentary or Law Commission reports where appropriate.

Structure is vital for essays. An introduction should set out your argument (your thesis statement). Each body paragraph should develop a specific point, supported by legal authority and critical analysis. Use clear topic sentences. Conclude by summarizing your main arguments and reiterating your overall stance. Aim for a balanced discussion, acknowledging different perspectives, but always steer towards a well-reasoned, supported conclusion.

Strategic Revision: Active Recall and Spaced Repetition

Passive revision, like simply re-reading notes, is ineffective. For A-Level Law, you need active recall. After studying a topic, try to explain it aloud or write down everything you remember without looking at your notes. Use flashcards for key terms, case names, and statutory sections. Websites like Anki or Quizlet can be useful for spaced repetition, ensuring you revisit information at optimal intervals before you forget it.

Focus on understanding the relationships between different areas of law. How does the concept of causation in criminal law relate to causation in tort? How do judicial precedent and statutory interpretation interact within the English Legal System? Building these connections will not only deepen your understanding but also make your arguments more sophisticated in exams.

Mastering Exam Technique and Time Management

The A-Level Law exam is a race against the clock. Practice full past papers under timed conditions. This isn't just about finishing; it's about allocating appropriate time to each question based on its weighting and complexity. For example, if a question is worth 25 marks, ensure you're dedicating roughly 25% of your total exam time to it. Don't spend 45 minutes on a 15-mark question.

Before writing, spend 5-10 minutes planning your answer for each question. For problem questions, identify all issues and map out your IRAC structure. For essays, outline your thesis, main arguments, and supporting points. A well-structured, slightly less detailed answer will always score higher than a rambling, unstructured one. Learn to be concise and to the point; examiners are looking for clear legal analysis, not flowery prose.

Leveraging Examiner Reports and Mark Schemes

These are goldmines of information. Examiner reports highlight common mistakes, areas where students underperform, and examples of good practice. They often provide insights into what examiners are *really* looking for in top-scoring answers. Pay close attention to the specific command words used in questions (e.g., 'evaluate', 'analyse', 'discuss', 'advise') and how examiner reports suggest addressing them.

Mark schemes aren't just about points for correct answers; they show the breakdown of marks for knowledge, application, analysis, and evaluation. Understand how marks are allocated for different skills. For instance, if a significant portion of marks is for 'critical evaluation', ensure your essays go beyond mere description. Use them to self-assess your practice answers and identify areas for improvement.

The Value of Broader Reading and Current Affairs

While not strictly required, engaging with current legal news and broader legal scholarship can significantly enhance your answers, especially in essays. Reading articles from The Guardian's legal section, The Lawyer, or even academic journals like the Modern Law Review (if you're feeling ambitious) can provide contemporary examples and different perspectives on legal issues. This demonstrates a genuine interest and a deeper understanding beyond the textbook.

Being aware of recent legal reforms or ongoing debates (e.g., reforms to the Human Rights Act, changes in sentencing guidelines) can add a layer of sophistication to your arguments. It shows you understand that law is not static but a dynamic field constantly evolving. This can be particularly impactful for universities like Cambridge, which value intellectual curiosity and an awareness of the broader context.

The takeaway

Achieving an A* in A-Level Law requires a multi-faceted approach: deep conceptual understanding beyond rote memorization, rigorous application of legal principles using methods like IRAC, sophisticated critical analysis in essays, strategic active recall revision, and precise exam technique honed through timed practice. By consistently focusing on these areas and leveraging available resources, you can not only secure top marks but also develop valuable analytical skills for university and beyond.
