How to Get an A* in A-Level English Literature

Howard Chan (IB 45, Cambridge HSPS) shares his specific strategies for achieving an A* in A-Level English Literature, based on his international school experience.

When I was applying to Cambridge, my English Literature grades were a crucial part of my application. I was doing the IB Diploma, but many of my friends were tackling A-Levels, and English Literature was a common pain point. It's not just about reading books; it's about developing a sophisticated analytical voice under exam conditions. Having gone through a similar rigorous literary analysis process for IB English Literature HL (where I scored a 7), I've seen what truly distinguishes top-tier responses.

This guide isn't about generic advice like 'read the book.' It's about the specific, actionable strategies that elevate your essays from good to exceptional, drawing parallels from my own high-level literary study and observations of successful A-Level students. We'll cover everything from pre-reading to exam technique, focusing on the nuances that examiners look for, whether you're aiming for UK, US, or Hong Kong universities.

Mastering the Text: Beyond Plot Summaries

Before you can analyze, you must know your texts inside out. This means more than just understanding the plot. For a play like Shakespeare's 'Othello' or a novel like 'Frankenstein', you need to be intimately familiar with character arcs, key themes, significant literary devices (imagery, metaphor, symbolism), and crucial turning points. I found that creating detailed chapter/scene summaries, noting down significant quotes as I read, and even drawing mind maps for character relationships were invaluable. Don't just read once; re-read sections, particularly those known for their complexity or thematic weight.

Crucially, understand the *context* in which the text was written. For 'The Handmaid's Tale', knowing about second-wave feminism and the rise of the religious right in the US is vital. For Romantic poetry, understanding the historical and philosophical shifts of the era is non-negotiable. This isn't about regurgitating facts; it's about using context to deepen your understanding of the author's purpose and the text's reception. A common mistake is to treat context as an add-on; it should be integrated seamlessly into your analysis to explain *why* something is significant.

Deconstructing the Prompt: Identifying the Nuance

An A* essay starts with a precise understanding of the question. Don't skim. Underline keywords, identify the core argument the question is asking you to address, and look for any implied tensions or debates. For example, a question like 'Explore the presentation of power in '1984'' is broad, but 'To what extent does Orwell present power as inherently corrupting in '1984'?' requires a more nuanced, argumentative approach. The 'to what extent' demands a balanced discussion, acknowledging complexities rather than just listing examples of power.

Before writing, take 5-10 minutes to plan. Brainstorm your main arguments (typically 2-3 per essay), select the strongest supporting quotes, and outline how each paragraph will contribute to your overall thesis. This pre-computation prevents rambling and ensures a coherent, focused argument. I always mapped out my paragraph topics and which specific quotes I'd use, ensuring variety and precision.

Crafting a Thesis: Your Argument's Backbone

Your thesis statement is the most important sentence in your essay. It's not a summary of the text or a restatement of the prompt. It's your specific, arguable answer to the question, which you will then prove throughout your essay. An A* thesis is sophisticated and often acknowledges complexity or nuance. Instead of 'Shakespeare shows that Othello is jealous,' aim for something like: 'While Othello's jealousy is undeniably a catalyst for tragedy, Shakespeare subtly implicates societal pressures and racial prejudice as equally destructive forces contributing to his downfall.'

Practice writing thesis statements for various past paper questions. Get feedback from your teacher. A strong thesis provides a clear roadmap for your essay and immediately signals to the examiner that you have a sophisticated understanding of the text and the question.

Analytical Depth: The 'How' and 'Why' of Quotes

The biggest differentiator between a B and an A* is the depth of analysis. It's not enough to just drop a quote and explain what it means. You need to analyze *how* the author achieves their effect and *why* it's significant in relation to the question. This involves close textual analysis: identifying literary devices (metaphor, simile, juxtaposition, foreshadowing, pathetic fallacy), discussing word choice (diction), sentence structure, and their cumulative impact. For instance, don't just say 'Lady Macbeth says 'unsex me here'.' Analyze the verb 'unsex,' its violent connotations, and how it reveals her desperate desire to shed feminine weakness for masculine ruthlessness, connecting it to themes of gender and power.

Think of it as zooming in on a specific phrase or word, dissecting its layers of meaning, and then zooming out to explain its broader implications for the text's themes and the author's message. Always link your analysis back to your thesis statement and the question. Every piece of analysis should serve to prove your central argument.

Structuring for Impact: Cohesion and Flow

An A* essay is logically structured and flows seamlessly. Each paragraph should have a clear topic sentence that directly supports your thesis. Follow the PEE or PEEL structure (Point, Evidence, Explanation/Elaboration, Link) but don't let it become formulaic. Vary your sentence structures and incorporate sophisticated transitions between paragraphs to create a cohesive argument. For example, instead of starting every paragraph with 'Firstly,' or 'Secondly,' use phrases like 'Building on this idea,' or 'In contrast to X, the text also explores Y.'

Ensure your introduction sets the stage and presents your thesis, your body paragraphs develop distinct arguments with evidence and analysis, and your conclusion synthesizes your arguments, reiterates your refined thesis, and offers a final insightful thought without introducing new information. A strong conclusion doesn't just summarize; it leaves the examiner with a lasting impression of your nuanced understanding.

Refining Language: Precision and Sophistication

Your vocabulary and writing style are critical. While clarity is paramount, an A* essay employs a sophisticated academic register. Use precise literary terminology correctly and integrate it naturally. Avoid colloquialisms or overly simplistic language. For example, instead of 'the author shows,' try 'the author elucidates,' 'delineates,' or 'problematizes.' However, never use a word you don't fully understand; authenticity is key.

Practice crafting complex sentences that convey nuanced ideas without becoming convoluted. Read academic literary criticism to observe how scholars articulate their arguments. Pay attention to syntax, punctuation, and grammar. Errors here, even minor ones, detract from your overall impression of sophistication. Proofread meticulously, even if it means sacrificing a minute or two of writing time in the exam.

Exam Strategy: Time Management and Stress Reduction

A-Level English Literature exams are demanding, often requiring two or three essays in a limited timeframe. Practise timed essays religiously. When I was preparing for my IB exams, I'd do at least one full timed paper every week in the months leading up to the exam. This builds stamina and helps you internalize the pacing required. Allocate time for planning (5-10 min), writing (35-40 min per essay), and proofreading (5 min). Stick to these allocations, even if it means leaving a paragraph slightly less developed.

Under exam pressure, it's easy to panic and deviate from your plan. Trust your preparation. If you've planned well, you have a roadmap. If you get stuck on a word or phrase, move on and come back to it. The goal is to produce a complete, coherent, and analytical essay, not a perfect first draft. Remember, examiners are looking for your ability to construct an argument under pressure, not just your knowledge of the text.

The takeaway

Achieving an A* in A-Level English Literature is about more than just reading books; it's a rigorous process of developing sophisticated analytical skills, crafting precise arguments, and executing under exam conditions. By mastering textual knowledge, deconstructing prompts, refining your thesis, deepening your analysis of quotes, structuring your essays coherently, and using precise language, you can elevate your responses from good to exceptional. Consistent practice, especially timed essays, will solidify these strategies and build the confidence needed to excel.
