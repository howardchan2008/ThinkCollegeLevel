How to Get an A* in A-Level Art & Design

From an IB 45 student: Howard Chan shares his concrete, non-generic strategies for achieving an A* in A-Level Art & Design. Learn real exam tips & timelines.

Getting an A* in A-Level Art & Design isn't just about being 'naturally talented.' It's about strategic planning, rigorous execution, and understanding exactly what examiners are looking for. As someone who navigated the IB Visual Arts HL curriculum alongside my UK university applications – securing an offer for HSPS at Cambridge (Peterhouse) – I know the pressure of balancing creative pursuits with academic demands. While my specific qualification was IB Visual Arts, the core principles for achieving top grades in a portfolio-based art subject are strikingly similar to A-Level Art & Design, particularly for CIE and Edexcel specifications.

This isn't a guide filled with vague advice like 'be creative.' I'll break down the process into actionable steps, drawing from my own experience in an international school in Tokyo, where I honed my portfolio for competitive university admissions. Whether you're aiming for a Russell Group university, an art foundation course, or simply want that A* on your transcript, these insights will help you refine your approach, manage your time, and produce a portfolio that truly stands out.

Deconstructing the Assessment Objectives (AOs)

Before you even pick up a brush, you need to internalize the four Assessment Objectives (AOs). For most A-Level Art & Design specifications (like CIE 9479 or Edexcel 9AD0), these are consistent: AO1: Develop ideas through sustained and focused investigations informed by contextual and other sources, demonstrating analytical and critical understanding. AO2: Refine ideas through experimenting and selecting appropriate resources, media, materials, techniques and processes. AO3: Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions in visual and/or other forms. AO4: Present a personal and meaningful response that realizes intentions and demonstrates understanding of visual language.

Think of them as a rubric for your entire portfolio. An A* isn't awarded for just one brilliant piece; it's for demonstrating excellence across all four AOs consistently. My approach was to have a mental checklist for each AO at every stage of my project. For instance, when researching (AO1), I wasn't just collecting images; I was critically analysing artists, linking them to my theme, and explaining *why* they were relevant to my developing ideas. This depth of analysis is crucial for distinguishing an A from an A*.

Choosing a Powerful Theme and Sustaining Investigation (AO1)

Your theme isn't just a topic; it's the backbone of your entire project. Avoid overly broad or generic themes like 'nature' or 'portraits.' Instead, find something specific, personal, and with enough depth to sustain a year-long investigation. For my IB Visual Arts Extended Essay, I explored 'The Deconstruction of Identity in Urban Spaces,' which allowed for diverse interpretations through photography, mixed media, and installation. This specificity gave me a clear direction.

For AO1, deep contextual research is non-negotiable. Don't just list artists; engage with their work. Analyze their techniques, their conceptual underpinnings, and critically compare them. For example, if exploring 'memory,' you might look at Christian Boltanski for his use of archival materials, then contrast that with the ephemeral nature of Felix Gonzalez-Torres's candy spills. Show how these artists *inform* your own practice, not just inspire it. This critical engagement demonstrates intellectual curiosity and a sophisticated understanding of art history and theory.

Strategic Experimentation and Refinement (AO2)

AO2 is where many students fall short by simply trying different materials without clear intent. An A* portfolio demonstrates *strategic* experimentation. Every experiment, every material choice, every technique explored should be a deliberate step towards refining your ideas. Document your process rigorously. Why did you choose acrylic over oil for a particular effect? What did you learn from that monoprinting session that informed your next piece?

Show failures as much as successes. A sketchbook filled with 'perfect' pieces looks suspicious. Examiners want to see your thought process, your problem-solving. If a technique didn't work, explain why and what you tried next. This iterative process of experimentation, reflection, and refinement is what AO2 is all about. It's not about producing a finished piece immediately; it's about the journey of discovery and mastery.

Effective Recording and Documentation (AO3)

Your sketchbook (or digital equivalent) is your most valuable asset for AO3. It's not just a collection of drawings; it's a visual diary of your artistic journey. Use it to record observations, generate ideas, sketch compositions, and document experiments. For an A*, your recording should be diverse and insightful. Don't just draw; use photography, collage, annotations, mind maps, and written reflections.

Crucially, link your recordings explicitly to your intentions. If you're sketching a street scene, explain how the light or the architecture relates to your theme of 'urban isolation.' Annotate your work with critical self-reflection. What worked? What didn't? What will you do next? This continuous dialogue with your own work demonstrates a high level of analytical thinking and self-awareness.

Developing a Personal and Meaningful Response (AO4)

AO4 is the culmination – your final outcome(s) and how they realize your intentions. An A* response isn't just technically proficient; it's personal, meaningful, and demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of visual language. This means your work should clearly communicate your ideas and intentions, showing a strong conceptual link back to your initial theme and research.

Your final pieces should feel like a natural progression from your sketchbook work, not a sudden, disconnected leap. The coherence between your developmental work and your final outcome is key. Ensure your presentation is professional and thoughtful. For example, if you're creating a series, consider how they interact visually. The 'meaningful' aspect often comes from a genuine connection to your own experiences or perspectives, rather than simply replicating existing styles.

Time Management and Portfolio Cohesion

The A-Level Art & Design course is a marathon, not a sprint. Procrastination is the enemy. I learned this the hard way during my IB Visual Arts process, where the final submission deadline felt like a sudden cliff edge. Break down your project into smaller, manageable chunks. Set mini-deadlines for research, experimentation phases, and developing specific pieces. Regular check-ins with your teacher are invaluable – they know the mark scheme inside out.

Crucially, your entire portfolio needs to tell a cohesive story. While you might explore different media or techniques, they should all relate back to your central theme. Imagine your portfolio as a visual essay, with each piece and sketchbook page contributing to a larger argument or exploration. This cohesion is a hallmark of an A* portfolio and demonstrates a sophisticated level of planning and conceptual understanding.

The Exam Component: Strategic Preparation

For the externally assessed component (often a sustained project over a set period, culminating in a timed exam), strategic preparation is paramount. When you receive the exam paper, don't just pick the first theme that sounds interesting. Spend significant time brainstorming and mind-mapping your chosen theme. Link it back to artists you've studied, techniques you've mastered, and concepts you've explored in your coursework. This initial planning phase, often overlooked, is critical for laying a strong foundation for your response.

During the timed exam itself, focus on demonstrating all four AOs. Don't just produce a pretty picture. Show your development, your experimentation, and your critical thinking through annotations and multiple studies. Even if your final piece isn't 'finished' in the traditional sense, the *process* you demonstrate within the exam conditions – the exploration, refinement, and personal response – will be heavily weighted. Practice working under timed conditions to build stamina and efficiency.

The takeaway

Achieving an A* in A-Level Art & Design is a testament to disciplined creativity, not just innate talent. By meticulously addressing each Assessment Objective, maintaining a rigorous and reflective sketchbook, and managing your time effectively, you can build a portfolio that is both personally meaningful and academically outstanding. Remember, the journey of artistic exploration and critical self-reflection is what truly elevates your work to the highest level.
