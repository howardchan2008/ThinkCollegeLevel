IB Visual Arts Extended Essay: Topics & Structure

Howard Chan, Cambridge HSPS, shares specific, non-generic advice for the IB Visual Arts Extended Essay. Learn about topic selection, structure, and avoiding common pitfalls for UK/US/HK applicants.

The IB Visual Arts Extended Essay (EE) can feel like an enigma, especially compared to the more 'academic' subjects. When I was tackling mine at an international school in Tokyo, aiming for Cambridge, HKU, and HKUST, I found precious little concrete advice. Most resources were generic, failing to address the unique blend of practical art and rigorous research this EE demands. I managed to score full marks, contributing to my predicted 45, and I genuinely believe my approach to topic selection and structural clarity was key. This isn't about 'being creative'; it's about being strategic.

Unlike a History or English EE, the Visual Arts EE isn't just about text; it's about visual analysis, contextualisation, and often, personal artistic practice. My own EE explored the representation of memory in contemporary Japanese photography, specifically through the works of Daido Moriyama and Shomei Tomatsu, and how their methods resonated with my own photographic series on urban decay. This dual focus—external analysis and internal reflection—is crucial. Here, I'll break down how to choose a compelling topic and structure your essay for maximum impact, drawing directly from my experience and what examiners truly look for.

Choosing Your Topic: The Intersection of Passion and Research

The biggest mistake you can make is picking a topic solely because it 'sounds academic' or because you think it will impress. The Visual Arts EE is a marathon, and genuine interest is your fuel. My advice: start with something you're already passionate about, perhaps an artist, movement, or medium that genuinely fascinates you. For me, that was Japanese street photography and the concept of 'are-bure-boke' (rough, blurred, out-of-focus) in post-war imagery. Don't be afraid to be specific; 'The use of colour in Van Gogh' is too broad. 'The psychological impact of blue in Picasso's Blue Period portraits' is much better.

Crucially, your topic must have an *investigative question* that allows for analysis, not just description. 'How did Yayoi Kusama use polka dots?' is descriptive. 'To what extent do Yayoi Kusama's polka dot motifs challenge traditional notions of selfhood and infinity in her immersive installations?' is analytical and researchable. Ensure there's enough scholarly material available—books, journal articles, exhibition catalogues—to support your argument. A niche topic with no academic discourse will leave you stranded. Conversely, an overly popular topic might make it hard to find a unique angle.

The Research Question: Your Guiding Star

Your research question (RQ) is the backbone of your entire EE. It needs to be clear, focused, and arguable. Avoid RQs that can be answered with a simple 'yes' or 'no,' or those that are purely factual. For instance, 'When did Cubism emerge?' is not an EE question. 'How did the deconstruction of perspective in early Cubist works by Braque and Picasso reflect and influence contemporary philosophical shifts regarding objective reality?' is. My own RQ revolved around how specific photographic techniques conveyed memory, allowing me to analyse both established artists and my own work.

Refine your RQ with your supervisor early on. It will likely evolve. My initial RQ was far too broad, trying to cover all of Japanese photography. Through discussions, I narrowed it down to specific artists and their engagement with memory, making it manageable and allowing for deeper analysis. A good RQ will naturally lead to a structured argument, dictating what evidence you need to gather and how you'll present it.

Structure: A Logical Flow for Complex Ideas

The Visual Arts EE, like any academic essay, demands a clear, logical structure. Think of it as a journey for your reader. I followed a standard academic essay structure, adapted for visual analysis:

<ol><li><b>Introduction:</b> Hook, background, context, and your precise research question. Briefly outline your argument and the scope of your essay.</li><li><b>Methodology/Contextualisation:</b> If applicable, explain your analytical framework or the specific historical/cultural context of your chosen artworks. For example, if you're analysing feminist art, briefly explain feminist art theory.</li><li><b>Body Paragraphs (Thematic/Artist-Specific):</b> This is where you conduct your visual analysis. Each paragraph should focus on a specific artwork, artist, or theme, and directly link back to your RQ. Use formal elements (composition, colour, line, texture, form, space) and interpret their meaning within the context of your argument. This is where you demonstrate your critical thinking.</li><li><b>Personal Practice (Optional but Recommended):</b> If your topic allows, integrate your own artistic practice. Discuss how your understanding of the artists/themes you've researched has informed your own work, or how your work serves as a case study for your RQ. This was a significant part of my EE and helped demonstrate a deeper engagement.</li><li><b>Conclusion:</b> Reiterate your thesis in new words, summarise your main findings, and answer your research question definitively. Discuss the implications of your findings and suggest avenues for further research.</li></ol>

Remember, transitions between paragraphs are crucial for maintaining flow. Each paragraph should build upon the last, guiding the reader through your argument seamlessly. Don't just list observations; analyse them critically.

Visual Analysis: Beyond Description

This is where many Visual Arts EEs fall short. It's not enough to say 'the painting has a lot of red.' You need to analyse *why* the artist used red, *what effect* it creates, and *how* it contributes to the artwork's meaning in relation to your RQ. Use specific art historical terminology accurately. For example, instead of 'dark colours,' you might discuss 'chiaroscuro' or 'tenebrism' if appropriate.

Integrate high-quality images of the artworks you are discussing. These are essential evidence. Ensure they are correctly captioned (artist, title, year, medium, dimensions, current location if known) and referenced in your bibliography. Your analysis should always refer back to these images, guiding the reader's eye to specific details you're discussing. For example, 'As seen in Figure 1, the fragmented perspective in Picasso's 'Les Demoiselles d'Avignon' (1907) challenges traditional Renaissance spatial conventions, suggesting a nascent Cubist approach to representing multiple viewpoints simultaneously.'

Integrating Your Own Artistic Practice (If Applicable)

If your EE topic allows for it, incorporating your own artistic practice can elevate your essay significantly. This demonstrates a deeper, experiential understanding of the concepts you're researching. I found this particularly rewarding as it allowed me to connect academic theory with my hands-on experience in photography. This isn't about showcasing your best artwork; it's about using your work as a case study or a point of reflection.

When integrating your practice, clearly articulate how your work relates to your research question and the artists/theories you've discussed. For example, 'Inspired by Moriyama's raw, grainy aesthetic, my series 'Tokyo Ghosts' (Figure X) explores the transient nature of urban memory through similar photographic techniques, aiming to evoke a sense of disorientation and nostalgia.' You'll need to include images of your own work, captioned and referenced just like external artworks, and critically reflect on your process and outcomes.

Referencing and Academic Honesty

Accurate and consistent referencing is non-negotiable. Whether you use MLA, Chicago, or another recognised style, stick to it rigorously. Every quote, every idea that isn't your own, must be cited. This demonstrates academic integrity and allows examiners to verify your sources. For Visual Arts, this also extends to image captions and their sources.

A comprehensive bibliography is essential. It shows the breadth of your research and supports the credibility of your arguments. Don't just list websites; include books, journal articles, exhibition catalogues, and interviews. Remember, quality of sources matters. Peer-reviewed journals and academic presses carry more weight than general interest blogs.

The Supervisor Relationship: Your Most Valuable Resource

Your EE supervisor is not just there to sign off on your work; they are your mentor. Schedule regular meetings, come prepared with specific questions, and be open to constructive criticism. My supervisor was instrumental in helping me narrow my research question and refine my visual analysis techniques. Don't wait until the last minute to seek their advice.

They can guide you on suitable resources, help you structure your arguments, and point out areas where your analysis might be weak or unclear. Remember, they have experience with the EE process and understand what examiners are looking for. Utilise their expertise fully, but also take ownership of your essay. It's *your* research, *your* argument, and *your* voice.

The takeaway

The IB Visual Arts Extended Essay is a unique opportunity to blend academic rigor with artistic passion. Success hinges on selecting a focused, researchable topic, formulating a precise research question, and structuring your essay logically with rigorous visual analysis. Don't shy away from integrating your own artistic practice if it enhances your argument. Remember, your supervisor is a critical resource, and meticulous referencing is paramount. Approach it strategically, and you'll not only achieve a high score but also deepen your understanding of the visual world.
