How to Get an A* in A-Level Geography

Achieve an A* in A-Level Geography with this guide from an IB 45 student admitted to Cambridge. Concrete strategies, exam tips, and resource recommendations.

When I was preparing for my A-Level equivalent exams (the IB Diploma), Geography was one of my favourite subjects, and I ended up scoring a 7, which is comparable to an A* at A-Level. While the IB and A-Level syllabi have differences, the core skills and strategies for excelling in a humanities subject like Geography are remarkably similar. This guide distills what I learned through my own journey, from an international school in Tokyo, applying to competitive universities like Cambridge, HKU, and HKUST.

Getting an A* in A-Level Geography isn't just about memorising facts; it's about understanding complex interconnections, developing robust analytical skills, and articulating your arguments with clarity and precision. This guide will walk you through my approach, covering everything from mastering content to acing your exams and independent research.

Mastering the Content: Beyond the Textbook

Your textbook is a starting point, not the destination. To truly master A-Level Geography content, you need to engage with it actively. For topics like 'Tectonic Processes and Hazards' or 'Global Governance,' I found it incredibly useful to supplement my textbook readings with academic articles (JSTOR is your friend, often accessible through school libraries) and reputable news sources like The Economist, The Guardian, and BBC News. This not only deepens your understanding but also provides contemporary case studies, which are crucial for higher marks.

Create detailed notes, but don't just copy. Instead, summarise key concepts, draw diagrams, and, most importantly, link different topics together. For example, how do climate change impacts (Paper 1) influence migration patterns (Paper 2)? These interconnections are what examiners look for. Flashcards are excellent for definitions and specific examples, but ensure you understand the 'why' behind each concept, not just the 'what'.

Developing Robust Case Studies

Case studies are the backbone of A-Level Geography essays. Generic examples will only get you so far. For an A*, you need specific, detailed, and well-analysed case studies. For instance, instead of just 'a volcanic eruption,' discuss the 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption, its specific impacts on air travel and the Icelandic economy, and the response strategies. For urban regeneration, compare and contrast London Docklands with a similar project in a developing country, highlighting different socio-economic contexts.

I recommend creating a dedicated 'Case Study Bank' for each major topic. Include location, date, specific facts and figures (e.g., number of deaths, economic cost, percentage increase/decrease), and a brief analysis of its relevance to different geographical concepts. Regularly review and update this bank with new developments – current events are a goldmine for fresh examples.

Acing the Exam Technique: Structure and Argument

Knowing the content is half the battle; presenting it effectively under exam conditions is the other. For essay-based questions, structure is paramount. I always used a clear introduction (defining terms, outlining argument), several body paragraphs (each focusing on a specific point with evidence/case study and explanation), and a strong conclusion (summarising, evaluating, and offering a nuanced perspective). Practice writing timed essays regularly.

Examiners want to see critical thinking. Don't just present information; evaluate it. Discuss strengths and weaknesses of different approaches, consider multiple perspectives (e.g., environmental, economic, social), and acknowledge complexities. Use connectives effectively (e.g., 'however,' 'furthermore,' 'consequently') to create a coherent and sophisticated argument. Remember to directly answer the question asked, avoiding 'information dumping' unrelated facts.

Mastering Data Response and Cartographic Skills

Data response questions often trip students up because they require a different skillset than essays. Focus on interpreting graphs, maps, and tables accurately. Identify trends, anomalies, and patterns. Always refer back to the data in your answers, quoting specific figures or observations. For example, 'Figure 1 shows a 15% increase in urban population between 1990 and 2010, suggesting rapid urbanisation in X region.'

Cartographic skills, including GIS interpretation and basic map work, are also vital. Understand different map projections, isolines, and how to describe distributions. Practice drawing simple sketch maps to illustrate concepts in your essays, if appropriate, but ensure they are clear and labelled correctly. The key is precision and attention to detail.

The Independent Investigation (NEA/Coursework)

The independent investigation (often called NEA or coursework) is your chance to shine and apply geographical skills. Choose a topic you are genuinely interested in and where you can collect primary data. My advice: start early. Planning, data collection, analysis, and writing up take significantly more time than you anticipate. For my IB Extended Essay (a similar independent research project), I spent months refining my research question and methodology.

Ensure your methodology is sound and justifiable. Consider ethical implications, sampling strategies, and potential limitations. Your analysis should go beyond description; interpret your findings, link them to geographical theory, and critically evaluate your own research process. This is where you demonstrate high-level academic skills, crucial for an A*.

Effective Revision Strategies

Passive revision (just re-reading notes) is inefficient. Active recall and spaced repetition are far more effective. Use techniques like blurting (writing down everything you remember about a topic from scratch) or teaching the material to someone else. Create mind maps to visualise connections between topics and identify gaps in your knowledge.

Past papers are your best friend. Start doing them well in advance of the exams, under timed conditions. Don't just do them; mark them rigorously using the mark schemes. Understand why marks are awarded and where you lost them. Identify recurring themes or question types. This iterative process of practice, self-assessment, and targeted revision is what truly hones your exam performance.

The takeaway

Achieving an A* in A-Level Geography demands more than rote memorisation; it requires deep conceptual understanding, rigorous analytical skills, and precise articulation. By actively engaging with content, developing robust case studies, mastering exam technique, and dedicating yourself to independent research, you can build a strong foundation for excellence. Consistent practice, critical self-assessment, and a genuine curiosity for the subject will be your greatest assets in securing that top grade.
